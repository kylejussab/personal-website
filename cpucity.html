Narrative Overview

CPU City follows the journey of Rhi Vyse, a determined teenager living in a vibrant city powered by technology. When the mysterious Pro-Krastor Nation begins to sabotage the city's systems, Rhi must step up to stop their plans by navigating the city to uncover its secrets; the Von Neumann Architecture that keeps it running. The educational adventure teaches computing concepts through a hero-driven narrative set in a colorful, fun, and engaging world.

BACKGROUND

Why?

The idea for CPU City came from a collaboration between myself and teammate Jess, a GCSE Computer Science teacher. While I was passionate about edutainment, Jess brought deep insight into the classroom struggles students faced. Together, we saw an opportunity to turn difficult concepts into something playful, accessible, and fun. Jess also provided direct access to our target audience, allowing for meaningful playtesting throughout development.

We envisioned our game as a long-term project; a gamified city where each level teaches a different part of the GCSE Computer Science syllabus. For this version, we focused on a single level covering the Von Neumann Architecture, one of the first topics students encounter. This choice allowed us to illustrate the game's full potential while keeping the project scoped and achievable.

PROGRAMMING

The feel of modern gaming

Quests and combat.

It was important to make this feel like a game you would play in 2024 and try to disguise the education part as much as possible because we believe education can be done passively and implicitly while having fun. I built both the quest and combat systems from the ground up to achieve this.

The quest system works in harmony with the dialogue system. Game objects are tagged as “quest givers,” and quests are assigned only if their index is one higher than the player’s current quest. This keeps the game linear and makes sure the player goes through a guided path while still being allowed to explore.

For combat, I developed a straightforward system that leverages basic enemy AI, utilizing state machines to transition between “idle,” “pursuing,” and “attacking” states. The AI follows the player using navigation meshes but is designed to be more irritating than intelligent, focusing on creating a challenging experience rather than sophisticated tactics.

The interconnected systems.

The world is designed to be visually engaging and filled with distractions, encouraging exploration and interaction with various collectible objects. This is balanced by a strict 10-minute time limit and a scoring system that rewards quick decision-making while penalizing distractions. The scoring system fosters friendly competition, making it ideal for classroom settings where students can replay the game to improve their scores. This replayability reinforces the educational content, helping students absorb key concepts more effectively.

The hydration system serves as a reminder of the importance of staying hydrated in real life. In-game, a lack of hydration restricts Rhi’s abilities, such as sprinting and using his attack fireball. Players must seek out water droplets to maintain hydration levels. Achieving high scores requires maintaining consistent hydration throughout the gameplay. This blend of collectible items, engaging world elements, and the added pressure of a time limit creates a dynamic and enjoyable balancing act, making the educational experience both fun and effective.
